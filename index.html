Abstract

Generative models have changed how machine learning represents complex data distributions, especially in language and vision, yet many real-world systems are observed instead as continuous, high-dimensional, and noisy sensor time series.

Existing generative modeling of sensor data remains fragmented across modalities, datasets, and task formulations. To address this gap, we introduce SensorGen, a large-scale study of sensor-signal generation spanning 14 settings across 4 domains, 7 datasets, and 12 signal modalities.

Leveraging SensorGen, we evaluate generative models from five major families and uncover three key findings: flow-matching models provide strong overall performance, signal properties shape which designs work, and generated signals have practical utility beyond visual realism.

Motivation

When, how, and why do generative models succeed or fail across diverse sensor time series generation settings?

Sensor signals vary widely in sampling frequency, time span, sequence length, channel structure, and physical semantics. These properties make direct adaptation from image or language generation non-trivial.

SensorGen Benchmark

SensorGen bridges the fragmented landscape of sensor time-series generation into a unified study platform. It studies generation as a joint problem over task formulations, sensing domains, signal properties, and model families.

Coverage

14 Generation Settings

Tasks are standardized under shared construction, training, and evaluation pipelines.

4 Real-World Domains

Emergency department, daily-life sensing, laboratory studies, and operation-room monitoring.

12 Signal Modalities

Bioelectrical, optical, motion, waveform, intervention, and longitudinal metabolic signals.

Data Regimes

Emergency Department

MIMIC-IV ECG provides clinical 12-lead electrocardiogram recordings paired with diagnostic reports.

Daily Life

CAPTURE-24, PPG-DaLiA, and Metabonet cover free-living wrist accelerometry, wearable physiological signals, and longitudinal metabolic traces.

Lab Study

PhyMER and SHHS provide controlled affective-state recordings and overnight polysomnography.

Operation Room

VitalDB provides intra-operative physiological waveforms, medication records, arterial blood pressure, PPG, ECG, and NIBP-derived measurements.

Task Taxonomy

Semantic-to-Signal

Generate realistic and semantically consistent signals from text, labels, or metadata.

Interpolation & Extrapolation

Predict future segments or reconstruct temporally incomplete signals from observed context.

Translation

Generate missing or hard-to-acquire target channels from accessible source channels.

Editing

Restore, refine, or counterfactually modify existing recordings while preserving non-target attributes.

Key Findings

SensorGen evaluates six representative models from five major generative families: diffusion, flow matching, autoregressive modeling, normalizing flows, and hierarchical generation.

Finding 1: Flow Matching Is a Strong Default

Flow-matching models provide strong overall performance across most sensor generation settings, making SiT a reliable baseline for heterogeneous sensor-signal generation.

Finding 2: Signal Properties Matter

Demographic covariates help longitudinal generation only when encoded carefully, while time-frequency modeling improves high-frequency signal generation.

Finding 3: Generated Signals Are Useful Beyond Realism

Synthetic sensor signals can improve downstream performance under data scarcity, and generation quality improves with additional training compute and model capacity.

Finding 4: Evaluation Must Reflect Sensor Structure

Visually plausible waveforms may still miss temporal, spectral, or physiological structure, motivating paired, distribution-level, and downstream utility evaluations.

Model Families

Diffusion

Iterative denoising from noise to data, instantiated with DiT.

Flow Matching

Continuous transport from noise to data, instantiated with SiT.

Autoregressive

Sequential token generation, instantiated with MAR.

Normalizing Flows

Likelihood-based generation through invertible transformations, instantiated with TarFlow.

Hierarchical

Multi-scale or coarse-to-fine synthesis, instantiated with FractalGen and Imagen.

Unified Protocol

Models are compared under shared task definitions, preprocessing, data splits, and evaluation protocols.

Main Results

All methods share unified task definitions, preprocessing pipelines, and data splits. For each method and setting, SensorGen generates 1,024 samples from held-out test conditions and reports task-specific metrics.

Aggregate Trends

Semantic-to-Signal

SiT achieves the best precision and recall, while DiT obtains the best aggregate FID in the current table.

Translation

SiT achieves the best aggregate MSE, MAE, SMSE, PSNR, and SSIM across translation tasks.

Interpolation & Extrapolation

SiT is strongest across aggregate paired-generation metrics, suggesting robust conditional generation.

Editing

Editing is more setting-dependent: Imagen is strong in training-required editing, while SiT performs best in training-free editing.

Analysis Study

This section separates deeper diagnostic studies from the main benchmark results. It focuses on downstream utility, sensor-specific design choices, scaling behavior, and failure modes.

Synthetic Data Utility

Synthetic ECG signals improve ECG-text pre-training transfer and supervised disease prediction under moderate augmentation. Excessive synthetic augmentation can degrade performance, likely due to generator artifacts or repeated patterns.

Demographic Conditioning

Raw demographic attributes can hurt longitudinal glucose generation, while normalized demographic covariates improve performance over the no-demographic baseline.

Long-Sequence Generation

Long sensor sequences are harder when generation depends only on semantic conditions, but target-signal context in imputation and translation can anchor generation.

High-Frequency Modeling

High-frequency signals such as EEG require spectral fidelity. Time-frequency modeling with STFT spectrograms improves high-frequency channel generation.

Normalization and Scaling

Range-based normalization provides a more stable target than z-score normalization in noisy physiological settings, and generation quality improves with training compute and model capacity.
